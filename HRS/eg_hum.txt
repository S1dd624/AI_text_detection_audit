Abstract— Mental health in modern society is a subject seldom
given importance. Dealing with issues such as stress, depression,
anxiety can be difficult unless help is sought and provided.
The Emotion-Aware Chatbot is a project we built with the goal
of providing said help. The chatbot applies to concepts such as
facial recognition, emotion detection, interacting with the user
via texts, processing natural language to assist in providing a
comfortable, safe experience. We aim for the chatbot to be
scalable, deployable and compatible in any environment to
encourage mental wellness and support.

Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD) is a public health concern,
critical if left untreated. According to estimates by The World
Health Organization (WHO), SAD has a 12.1% lifetime
prevalence [1], affecting over 75% of people in low-middle
income countries, with absolutely no services available.
Social stigmas and discriminatory practices aggravate this
problem, leading to no access for care [2][3].
The lack of attention and importance given to mental health
problems has been concerning. Yet there is hope to overcome
this, and Artificial Intelligence has been on the frontline to
spearhead this problem using groundbreaking discoveries,
models and methods.
Emotion-Aware Chatbots are a recent development in
tackling mental health issues. By applying natural language
processing (NLP), computer vision, deep learning, real-
time facial expression translation these chatbots are able to
provide appropriate responses [4]. Our project is a Flask-based
web application, based on conversational AI using Chatterbot,
combining OpenCV to detect emotions, and fetching from a
user's database to track emotions. Any cues, be it visual or text
can be interpreted by the chatbot. The user experience
improves with continuous usage as the chatbot can detect and
learn from emotional patterns, thus giving emphatic,
constructive responses.
Human-computer interaction (HCI) is a rapidly evolving
field. Strides have been made to make AI emotionally intelligent,
noticing minute non-verbal details as cues to be of better service
in medical interventions [5]. Using chatbots has shown reduced
feelings of isolation, helping users to open up, and making them
feel welcome and not isolated [6].
The COVID-19 pandemic serves as a landmark event for
emotion-aware chatbots. As the world saw an exponential rise in
demand for digital interactions, it was the call of the hour to
integrate artificial intelligence with healthcare, giving rise to the
tele-mental health industry.

II. EXISTING LITERATURE
• Ahmad et al. [4] proposed emotion-aware
chatbots using detection of sentiments. Our
chatbot is inspired from their framework.
• Inkster et al. [6] surveyed machine learning and
its applications in mental health diagnostics. A
similar classification has been undertaken by us.
• Mimicking human empathy for emphatic dialogue
was tackled by Ghosh et al. [7].
• Molla Hosseini et al. [8] introduced deep neural
network architecture: FER-2103 and CK+, for
attaining near human-level accuracy. Our chatbot
built upon their study of classifying complex facial
cues via convolutional architectures.
• Multi-modal fusion technique was employed by
Barros et. al [9] for interpreting facial and voice
data. Taking cues, our project also uses multiple
sensory modalities using visual and textual inputs.
• Chancellor et al. [10] applied natural language
processing (NLP) for analysing patterns in a
comprehensive manner for everyday
communication, also echoed in our chatbot's
approach to sentiment analysis.
• 'Woebot', was a chatbot built on Cognitive
Behavioural Therapy (CBT) by Fitzpatrick et
al. [11]. Depressive symptoms were reduced over
the span of two weeks, proving emotion-aware
AI's efficacy.
• Majumder et al. [12] developed a conversational
agent capable of switching strategies for
dialogue, to adjust to the user's personality.
Results showed significant increase in user
satisfaction and the agent's perceived empathy.
• Explainable AI (XAI) was ventured into by
Zhong et al. [13], integrating Shapley Additive
exPlanations (SHAP) and attention-based
visualization, supporting our adoption of XAI
components in our chatbot.
• Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique
(SMOTE) was introduced by Chawla et al. [18]
for class-imbalanced datasets. We have utilized
SMOTE in the development of our chatbot.

III. MATERIALS AND METHODS
A. Dataset
Our system is built on a dataset combining multiple modes
of interaction, to give a simulated experience that is as close
to real-world scenarios as possible. The dataset comprises of:
1. Textual Data: This consists of user-generated
messages manually labelled with sentiment classes
such as positive, negative, or neutral. Example
entries include phrases like “I feel sad,” “You’re
amazing,” and “This is horrible,” extracted from
actual chatbot sessions and augmented with
emotionally expressive content. These inputs were
inspired by techniques in sentiment classification
studies using real-life chat and social media
interactions [14].
2. Visual Data: Using the webcam module integrated
into the camera_stream.py and main.py files, real-
time facial expressions are captured during
conversation. Each frame is analysed using a CNN-
based emotion recognition model trained to classify
expressions into categories: happy, sad, angry,
fearful, and neutral. This process mimics datasets
like FER-2013 but operates in real-time to provide a
live feedback loop [15].
Together, these two sources create a hybrid dataset that
allows the chatbot to combine verbal cues and non-verbal
facial signals for a richer emotional understanding of the user.
B. Preprocessing of Dataset
We have given significant focus on preprocessing, for both
modes of data, text and visual. To eliminate noise, all text is
converted to lowercase, removing punctuations and stop words.
The textual data is then constructed in a structural manner,
applying tokenization through the Natural Language Toolkit
(NLTK). The text is segmented into individual units i.e tokens,
these tokens are converted into numerical vectors using Term
Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF). TF-IDF
weighs the importance of a term comparing it's occurrence
throughout. The model has thus been made to distinguish
relevant cues, downplaying less significant or informative
words [16]. The resulting TF-IDF vectors are utilized as input
for classifier training. The resulting TF-IDF vectors are stored
and utilized as input for classifier training.
The OpenCV library offers capturing webcam feed into real-
time frames for visual analysis. Each captured frame undergoes
grayscale conversion to reduce computational complexity while
preserving essential features. 

For high-dimensional inputs, building a model to be
interpretable requires feature selection. We tailored the
textual and visual domains to only retain relevant features,
leading to better classifier performance, reducing complexity
in computation and accurate predictions.
The textual domain had Term Frequency–Inverse
Document Frequency (TF-IDF) vectorization to initially
represent text, which converts raw text into a numerical
format while preserving the relative importance of each word.
The plethora of features possible were analysed via the Chi-
square (χ²) test for obtaining the independence between each
word and it's corresponding sentiment label (positive or
negative. Retainable features were shown to have higher scores
in the Chi-square test, due to their strong relation with the
target classes.

To evaluate the effectiveness of our Emotion-Aware Chatbot,
we collected metrics, quantitative as well as qualitative from
those that participated in interacting with the chatbot.
Quantitative feedback included classification accuracy, F1
score, UAC-ROC and other metrics. Multiple tests were run
from real-time emotion recognition, repeated simulations and
labeled datasets.
The system is designed not just to detect emotional states but
to respond empathetically and adaptively, especially in
high-risk emotional contexts such as loneliness, anxiety, and
suicidal ideation
